What counts as a drink? Understanding alcohol units and standard drinks

What is an alcohol unit?

Mindrink uses a simple, internationally recognised definition:

1 unit = 10 grams of pure alcohol

This makes it easy to compare any drink‚Äîbeer, wine, or spirits‚Äîon the same scale.

To calculate units for any drink:

Units = (Volume in ml √ó ABV% √ó 0.789) √∑ 10

Why 0.789?
Because ethanol weighs 0.789 g/ml, so this converts liquid volume into grams of alcohol.

üéØ Why Mindrink uses units instead of "drinks"

The problem with "drinks" is that they're not standardized:

  • A "glass of wine" can be 125 ml, 175 ml, or 250 ml
  • Beer ranges from 3% to 9%+ ABV
  • Cocktails vary by bar and recipe
  • Spirits can be single, double, or poured generously

Using units (10 g of alcohol) gives you a consistent and comparable measurement no matter what you drink.

This helps you:

  • Understand how much alcohol you're actually consuming
  • Compare different days, weeks, and sessions accurately
  • Stay aware of binge thresholds and guideline ranges
  • See long-term patterns more clearly

A note on different unit systems around the world

Not all countries define an "alcohol unit" the same way.
This can make online charts look inconsistent ‚Äî but it's simply because they use different amounts of pure alcohol per "unit" or "standard drink."

United Kingdom

1 unit = 10 ml pure alcohol (‚âà 8 g)
This is why UK examples (e.g., "2.1 units" for a glass of wine) don't match Mindrink.

USA

1 standard drink = 14 g pure alcohol
Used by NIAAA, CDC
Larger than both UK and WHO-style units.

Canada

1 standard drink = 13.45 g pure alcohol
Used in national guidelines and risk studies.

Mindrink (WHO-style system)

1 unit = 10 g pure alcohol
Matches systems used in WHO, Australia, Ireland, and many global research papers.

Why Mindrink uses the 10 g standard

Using 10 g per unit creates a global, consistent measure that works well across countries and aligns with most public-health research.

What this means for users

If you compare Mindrink with UK, US, or Canadian charts, numbers may look different ‚Äî
that's normal, because the underlying "unit" definition is not the same.
